## Ignore the spec sheet marketing and check these first

At this price point, the difference between a genuinely good phone and a frustrating one rarely shows up in the spec sheet's headline numbers. It shows up in a handful of specifics that budget listings tend to bury.

## Software update commitment

This matters more than almost anything else in this price range. A phone that only gets one Android version update and a year of security patches will feel outdated — and become a security risk — well before the hardware itself wears out. Check the manufacturer's specific update policy for the exact model, not just their general reputation.

## Actual RAM management, not just RAM size

Two phones can both list "8GB RAM" while behaving completely differently under real use, because budget chipsets vary hugely in how well they manage memory. Look for reviews that specifically test app re-opening speed — does the browser tab or app you had open five minutes ago need to fully reload, or does it resume instantly? That's the real-world RAM test.

## Display refresh rate

A 90Hz or 120Hz display makes everyday scrolling noticeably smoother than 60Hz, and it's one of the most reliably-detectable upgrades a budget phone can have. It's increasingly common even under $300 — don't settle for 60Hz if a 90Hz option exists nearby in price.

## Battery capacity relative to chipset efficiency

A bigger battery number doesn't automatically mean better battery life — an inefficient chipset can drain a large battery as fast as an efficient one drains a smaller battery. Look specifically for third-party screen-on-time tests rather than the manufacturer's own battery capacity number.

## Network bands, if you're buying internationally or switching carriers

A phone built primarily for one region can be missing specific bands used by carriers elsewhere, causing weaker signal or missing 5G entirely on your network even though the phone "supports 5G" generally. Check your specific carrier's supported bands against the phone's listed bands before buying, especially for imported models.

## The one-line checklist

- Update policy: at least 2 OS versions, 3+ years of security patches
- Refresh rate: 90Hz or higher if available near your price point
- Battery: real screen-on-time reviews, not just mAh
- Network bands: confirmed compatible with your specific carrier
- RAM behavior: check app re-open speed in reviews, not just the GB number
