## Why 30 days is enough — if it's structured

Cramming random problems for a month rarely works. Structured, deliberate practice across four focused weeks works far better than the same total hours spent unfocused.

## Week 1: Rebuild your fundamentals

Before solving problems, make sure the underlying data structures are automatic, not something you have to reconstruct from memory mid-interview: arrays, hash maps, linked lists, trees, and basic graph representations. Spend this week re-implementing each from scratch once, then reviewing time and space complexity for the common operations on each.

## Week 2: Pattern recognition, not problem memorization

Interview problems cluster into a fairly small number of recurring patterns: two pointers, sliding window, BFS/DFS, dynamic programming, and binary search on answer space. Instead of grinding random problems, work through 4–5 problems per pattern specifically, and after each one, write a one-sentence note on what *cue in the problem statement* should have told you which pattern to reach for. That cue-recognition is what actually transfers to a new problem you haven't seen.

## Week 3: Full mock interviews under real conditions

Practicing alone with unlimited time teaches different skills than performing live. This week, simulate the real thing:

- Solve with a 30–45 minute hard limit, out loud, as if explaining to an interviewer
- Practice with a peer or a mock-interview platform at least twice this week
- Explicitly practice the "I don't know where to start" moment — pick a problem you haven't seen, and practice narrating your thinking process even when you're stuck, since that's exactly what real interviews require

## Week 4: System design and behavioral prep, plus rest

For roles above entry-level, system design carries real weight. Learn the standard building blocks once — load balancers, caching layers, database replication, message queues — and practice sketching a design for a familiar product (a URL shortener, a chat app) out loud in 20 minutes.

Prepare 4–5 behavioral stories using a simple structure (situation, action, result) that can flex to answer "tell me about a conflict," "tell me about a failure," and "tell me about a time you led something," since most behavioral questions are variations on a small set of themes.

In the final 2–3 days, stop learning new material entirely. Review your notes, sleep properly, and go in rested — fatigue costs more performance at this stage than any last-minute cramming gains back.

## The habit that matters most across all four weeks

After every practice problem, whether you solved it or not, write down the specific thing that went wrong or right. A stack of solved problems teaches less than a shorter list of named, specific lessons.
