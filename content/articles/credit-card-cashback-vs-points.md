## They're solving different problems, not competing on the same axis

Cashback and reward points aren't really "better" or "worse" than each other in the abstract — they suit different spending habits and different amounts of effort you're willing to put in. Nothing here is personalized financial advice; check the terms of any specific card before applying.

## How cashback actually works

Cashback is straightforward by design: a percentage of what you spend comes back as statement credit or a direct deposit, usually somewhere between 1% and 5% depending on the category and the specific card. The value is fixed and known in advance — a dollar back is worth exactly a dollar, no interpretation required.

**Cashback tends to suit you better if:**
- You want to redeem value with zero research or strategy
- Your spending doesn't concentrate heavily in one category worth optimizing around
- You'd rather have flexible cash than be limited to specific redemption options

## How points and miles actually work

Points systems attach a variable, negotiable value to each point, which can swing significantly based on how you redeem them. The same points balance might be worth one amount redeemed for cash-equivalent statement credit, and considerably more transferred to an airline or hotel partner for the right booking. That upside is real, but it requires research, flexibility on travel dates, and comfort with a system that's intentionally more complex than cashback.

**Points tend to suit you better if:**
- You travel enough that airline or hotel transfers are realistic, not theoretical
- You're willing to spend time comparing redemption options instead of taking the first one offered
- You can treat a points balance as an asset to plan around rather than money you want in-hand immediately

## The comparison that actually matters

Don't compare a cashback card's percentage directly to a points card's "points per dollar" — they're different units. Instead, convert points to their realistic cash-equivalent value for redemptions *you would actually use*, not the best-case redemption in the card's marketing. If you're honest about your actual travel habits and that realistic value lands below what an equivalent cashback card would pay, cashback wins for you regardless of the points card's ceiling.

## One rule that applies to both

Neither is worth carrying a balance for. Any rewards rate is dwarfed by typical credit card interest within a month or two of carrying debt — rewards only make sense as a bonus on spending you were already going to pay off in full.
