## Why it's worth diversifying

ChatGPT is a fine default, but treating it as the only option means missing tools that are better suited to specific jobs — longer documents, tighter style control, or just a different free-tier limit when you've hit your quota for the day.

## Claude

Anthropic's Claude is particularly strong at long-document work: summarizing a 40-page PDF, holding a consistent voice across a long draft, or following a detailed style guide without drifting back to generic phrasing after a few paragraphs. The free tier resets daily and is generous enough for most personal writing tasks.

## Google Gemini

Gemini's biggest advantage is context window size — it can hold enormous documents in a single conversation, which matters if you're working from research notes, transcripts, or an existing manuscript you want it to reference throughout. It's also tightly integrated with Google Docs if that's already your workflow.

## Perplexity

Perplexity isn't primarily a writing tool, but it's the best of this group at grounding a draft in current information, since every claim comes with a linked source. Use it for the research pass, then move the verified facts into whichever tool you're using to actually write.

## Notion AI

If you already write inside Notion, its built-in AI saves the copy-paste round trip: it can draft, summarize, or rewrite a selection inline, without ever leaving the page you're already working in.

## Mistral's Le Chat

Le Chat is worth knowing about specifically for speed — responses come back fast enough that it barely interrupts a drafting flow, which matters more than raw quality for tasks like knocking out a first pass you'll heavily edit anyway.

## How to actually choose

Don't pick one and stick with it out of habit. Match the tool to the job:

- **Long reference documents** → Gemini or Claude
- **Anything that needs to cite real, current sources** → Perplexity
- **Quick inline edits inside existing notes** → Notion AI
- **Fast first drafts you'll heavily rewrite** → Le Chat

None of these replace editing. Treat every output as a first draft from a fast, tireless, occasionally-wrong collaborator — not a finished piece.
